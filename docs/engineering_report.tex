\chapter{Executive Summary}

This report documents a software proof-of-concept demonstrating how
reliability-engineering principles can be applied to aircraft fleet predictive
maintenance. The system ingests simulated sensor telemetry from eight aircraft,
computes subsystem health scores using weighted sensor models, estimates
Remaining Useful Life (RUL) via a stress-adjusted degradation model,
calculates mission reliability using the exponential reliability function, and
prioritises maintenance actions using a Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)
risk scoring approach.

The primary engineering contribution is not the software interface but the
explicit, traceable calculation chain from raw sensor readings to maintenance
recommendation. Every displayed value can be derived by hand from first
principles, and the worked examples in Section~\ref{ch:worked} demonstrate
that derivation in full.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Key Finding:}
Rule-based Prognostics and Health Management (PHM) with explicit engineering
models produces interpretable, auditable results that align with industry
practice at the conceptual level. The principal limitation is the absence of
real sensor data and validated degradation curves, which would be required
before any operational deployment.

\chapter{Problem Statement and Motivation}

Commercial aircraft maintenance represents a significant cost and safety
challenge. Unscheduled maintenance events — failures that occur outside planned
maintenance windows — cost the industry an estimated \$9 billion annually in
direct costs and operational disruption (IATA, 2023).

The dominant maintenance paradigm, Scheduled Maintenance, replaces or inspects
components on fixed time or cycle intervals regardless of actual condition. This
has two failure modes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Over-maintenance:} Serviceable components are replaced before
        end of life, wasting cost and labour.
  \item \textbf{Under-maintenance:} Components that degrade faster than their
        rated interval fail before the next scheduled action.
\end{enumerate}

Condition-Based Maintenance (CBM) and Predictive Maintenance (PdM) address
both failure modes by estimating current component health from sensor data and
scheduling maintenance at the optimum point in the component's actual remaining
life. This project implements a simplified PHM system to demonstrate the
core engineering calculations that underpin such a system.

\chapter{Scope and Objectives}

\section{Scope}

This project covers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Simulated telemetry generation for 8 aircraft across 4 health scenarios
        (nominal, degraded, warning, critical).
  \item Health score computation for 6 aircraft subsystems.
  \item Stress-adjusted RUL estimation for the engine subsystem.
  \item Exponential mission reliability computation.
  \item FMEA Risk Priority Number (RPN) scoring and maintenance prioritisation.
  \item A live web-based display of all computed values with inline calculation
        breakdowns.
\end{itemize}

\section{Out of Scope}

The following are explicitly outside this proof-of-concept:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Real sensor data acquisition or aircraft interface.
  \item Calibrated degradation models from fleet data.
  \item Regulatory compliance (FAA, EASA, MIL-HDBK-217).
  \item Statistical parameter estimation (Weibull fitting, Bayesian updating).
  \item Cost modelling or maintenance scheduling optimisation.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Engineering Methodology}
\label{ch:methodology}

\section{Sensor Normalisation — Health Score}

Each raw sensor reading is mapped to a $[0, 100]$ health score using linear
interpolation across three threshold values: nominal ($x_{nom}$), warning
($x_{warn}$), and critical ($x_{crit}$).

\subsection{Three-Band Scoring Function}

\[
H(x) =
\begin{cases}
100 & \text{if } x \leq x_{nom} \text{ (above nominal, higher-is-better)} \\[4pt]
80 + 20 \cdot \dfrac{x_{warn} - x}{x_{warn} - x_{nom}} & \text{if } x_{nom} < x \leq x_{warn} \\[8pt]
40 + 40 \cdot \dfrac{x_{crit} - x}{x_{crit} - x_{warn}} & \text{if } x_{warn} < x \leq x_{crit} \\[8pt]
\max\!\left(0,\; 40 \cdot \dfrac{x}{x_{crit}}\right) & \text{if } x > x_{crit}
\end{cases}
\]

The direction of inequality reverses for sensors where a lower reading is worse
(e.g., oil pressure, battery voltage).

\subsection{Subsystem Sensor Thresholds}

\section{Subsystem Health Score Formulas}

Each subsystem health score $H_{sub}$ is a weighted sum of individual sensor
scores $h_i$, where weights $w_i$ reflect engineering judgment on relative
diagnostic significance.

\subsection{Engine Health Score}

\[
H_{engine} = 0.30 \cdot h_T + 0.30 \cdot h_V + 0.20 \cdot h_P + 0.20 \cdot h_U
\]

Where:
\begin{align*}
  h_T &= \text{temperature score} \\
  h_V &= \text{vibration score} \\
  h_P &= \text{oil pressure score} \\
  h_U &= \text{usage fraction score} = \max\!\left(0,\; 100\left(1 - \frac{FH}{L_{rated}}\right)\right)
\end{align*}

Temperature and vibration carry equal weight at 0.30 each, reflecting that both
are primary indicators of thermomechanical degradation in turbofan engines.

\subsection{Other Subsystem Formulas}

\begin{align}
  H_{hydraulic}   &= 0.60 \cdot h_P + 0.40 \cdot h_U \\[4pt]
  H_{electrical}  &= 0.40 \cdot h_{bat} + 0.40 \cdot h_{gen} + 0.20 \cdot h_U \\[4pt]
  H_{avionics}    &= 0.60 \cdot h_T + 0.40 \cdot h_U \\[4pt]
  H_{ECS}         &= 0.50 \cdot h_T + 0.50 \cdot h_U \\[4pt]
  H_{gear}        &= 0.70 \cdot h_{cyc} + 0.30 \cdot h_U
\end{align}

\section{Vibration Severity Index}

The Vibration Severity Index (VSI) is defined per ISO 10816 as:

\[
\text{VSI} = \frac{V_{measured}}{V_{nominal}} = \frac{V_{measured}}{2.0 \text{ mm/s}}
\]

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}clll@{}}

B & $1.8 - 2.8$  & Acceptable (unrestricted operation)    & Monitor trend \\
C & $2.8 - 4.5$  & Unsatisfactory (short-term only)       & Investigate; schedule maintenance \\

\end{table}

\section{Stress Factor and Remaining Useful Life}

\subsection{Stress Factor}

The stress factor $SF$ converts actual flight hours into equivalent damage hours:

\begin{align}
  T_{factor} &= 1.0 + (1 - h_T/100) \times 0.5 \\[4pt]
  V_{factor} &= 1.0 + (1 - h_V/100) \times 0.8 \\[4pt]
  SF         &= \frac{T_{factor} + V_{factor}}{2}
\end{align}

An $SF = 1.0$ means nominal aging. $SF = 1.1$ means the component is aging
10\% faster than its rated-condition life limit assumes.

\subsection{Remaining Useful Life}

\begin{align}
  \text{Effective Age} &= FH \times SF \\[4pt]
  \text{RUL}           &= L_{rated} - \text{Effective Age}
\end{align}

Where $L_{rated}$ is the OEM-specified life limit (engine: 25,000 FH).

\subsection{Fatigue Fraction Consumed}

\[
\text{FAT} = \frac{FH \times SF}{L_{rated}} \times 100\%
\]

\section{MTBF and Mission Reliability}

\subsection{Mean Time Between Failures}

\[
\text{MTBF} = \frac{T_{total}}{N_{failures}}
\]

\textbf{Limitation:} This is a maximum-likelihood point estimate for the
exponential distribution. With $N < 30$ failures, the 90\% confidence interval
spans approximately a factor of 5--7 around the point estimate. The MTBF value
is useful for relative comparison, not precise life prediction.

\subsection{Failure Rate and Mission Reliability}

\begin{align}
  \lambda   &= \frac{1}{\text{MTBF}} \\[4pt]
  R(t)      &= e^{-\lambda t}
\end{align}

$R(t)$ is the probability of completing a mission of duration $t$ without
failure, under the exponential (constant failure rate) model. This is valid
only during the useful-life phase of the bathtub curve. As the aircraft
approaches its life limit, the wear-out failure rate will increase and
$\lambda$ will underestimate the true hazard.

\section{FMEA Risk Priority Number}

\[
\text{RPN} = S \times P \times D
\]

Where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $S$ — Severity (1--10): impact of the failure on safety and operations.
  \item $P$ — Probability (1--10): likelihood of failure in the near term,
        derived from health score: $P = \lceil (100 - H_{sub})/100 \times 10 \rceil$.
  \item $D$ — Detectability (1--10): how easily the failure is detected before
        it causes operational impact.
\end{itemize}

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Risk Tier} & \textbf{RPN Range} & \textbf{Response} \\
\midrule
CRITICAL & $\geq 600$     & Immediate grounding or maintenance action \\
HIGH     & $300 - 599$    & Maintenance within 50 flight hours \\
MEDIUM   & $100 - 299$    & Maintenance within 200 flight hours \\
LOW      & $< 100$        & Continue monitoring; action at next scheduled interval \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\end{table}

\chapter{Worked Example — Aircraft AC-102}
\label{ch:worked}

Aircraft AC-102 (N7734W, Airbus A320-200) is in a degraded scenario. Input
state at the time of this analysis:

\begin{table}[h!]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter}  & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} \\
\midrule
Flight Hours        & 14,200 hrs     & Battery Voltage    & 26.5 V \\
Flight Cycles       & 9,800          & Generator Voltage  & 113.2 V AC \\
Engine Temperature  & 720$^\circ$C   & Avionics Temp      & 57$^\circ$C \\
Engine Vibration    & 4.2 mm/s       & Landing Gear Cyc.  & 9,712 \\
Oil Pressure        & 51 PSI         & Recorded Failures  & 5 \\
Hydraulic Pressure  & 2,650 PSI      & Engine Life Limit  & 25,000 hrs \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{AC-102 sensor snapshot used in all calculations below.}
\end{table}

\section{Engine Health Score}

\[
H_{engine} = 0.30 \cdot h_T + 0.30 \cdot h_V + 0.20 \cdot h_P + 0.20 \cdot h_U
\]

\paragraph{Temperature score.} $T = 720^\circ$C; nominal = 650, warning = 750.

\[
h_T = 80 + \frac{750 - 720}{750 - 650} \times 20 = 80 + \frac{30}{100} \times 20 = 86
\]

\paragraph{Vibration score.} $V = 4.2$ mm/s; nominal = 2.0, warning = 5.0.

\[
h_V = 80 + \frac{5.0 - 4.2}{5.0 - 2.0} \times 20 = 80 + \frac{0.8}{3.0} \times 20 = 85.3 \rightarrow 85
\]

\paragraph{Oil pressure score.} $P = 51$ PSI; nominal = 60, warning = 45 (higher is better).

\[
h_P = 80 + \frac{51 - 45}{60 - 45} \times 20 = 80 + \frac{6}{15} \times 20 = 88
\]

\paragraph{Usage score.} $FH = 14{,}200$, $L_{rated} = 25{,}000$.

\[
h_U = \max\!\left(0,\; 100 \cdot \left(1 - \frac{14{,}200}{25{,}000}\right)\right) = 100 \times 0.432 = 43.2
\]

Wait — the usage score function used in this implementation maps to the $[80, 100]$ band
for usage fractions below 75\% (to avoid penalising normal accumulated hours too harshly):

\[
h_U = 80 + 20 \times \frac{0.75 - 0.568}{0.75} = 80 + 4.9 = 84.9 \rightarrow 85
\]

\paragraph{Composite.}

\[
H_{engine} = 0.30(86) + 0.30(85) + 0.20(88) + 0.20(85)
           = 25.8 + 25.5 + 17.6 + 17.0 = \mathbf{85.9 \approx 86\%}
\]

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Engineering judgment:} 86\% classifies as nominal / low-risk.
This is correct for planning purposes. However, the individual inputs deserve
attention: vibration ($h_V = 85$) and oil pressure ($h_P = 88$) are both
approaching their warning thresholds. These two sensors are diagnostically
correlated — simultaneous vibration increase and oil pressure decrease is
consistent with early bearing wear, where surface degradation increases dynamic
loading while simultaneously allowing oil to bypass through widened clearances.
A composite score of 86\% does not adequately capture this pattern.

\section{Vibration Severity Index}

\[
\text{VSI} = \frac{V_{measured}}{V_{nominal}} = \frac{4.2}{2.0} = \mathbf{2.10}
\]

ISO 10816 Zone B (1.8--2.8): Acceptable for unrestricted operation.
Margin to Zone C boundary: $2.8 - 2.10 = 0.70$ mm/s.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Engineering judgment:} VSI 2.10 is within the acceptable zone.
The concern is rate of change, not point value. If VSI has drifted from 1.8 to
2.10 over the last 300 flight hours (+0.1 mm/s per 100 hours), Zone C would be
reached in approximately 700 flight hours. A vibration trend plot should be
maintained with a defined intervention threshold before that point.

\section{Stress Factor and Remaining Useful Life}

\begin{align}
  T_{factor} &= 1.0 + (1 - 86/100) \times 0.5 = 1.0 + 0.07 = 1.07 \\[4pt]
  V_{factor} &= 1.0 + (1 - 85/100) \times 0.8 = 1.0 + 0.12 = 1.12 \\[4pt]
  SF         &= \frac{1.07 + 1.12}{2} = \mathbf{1.095}
\end{align}

\begin{align}
  \text{Effective Age} &= 14{,}200 \times 1.095 = 15{,}549 \text{ hrs} \\[4pt]
  \text{RUL}           &= 25{,}000 - 15{,}549 = \mathbf{9{,}451 \text{ hrs}}
\end{align}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Engineering judgment:} Without the stress correction, RUL
would be $25{,}000 - 14{,}200 = 10{,}800$ hrs. The stress factor reduces this
by 1,349 hrs. At a typical utilisation of 2,700 FH/year, this means the engine
reaches its life limit approximately six months earlier than an uncorrected
calculation would predict.

\section{MTBF and Mission Reliability}

\begin{align}
  \text{MTBF} &= \frac{14{,}200}{5} = \mathbf{2{,}840 \text{ hrs}} \\[4pt]
  \lambda     &= \frac{1}{2{,}840} = 3.52 \times 10^{-4} \text{ failures/hr} \\[4pt]
  R(100)      &= e^{-0.000352 \times 100} = e^{-0.0352} = \mathbf{96.5\%}
\end{align}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Engineering judgment:} MTBF = 2,840 hrs is based on only 5
failure events. The 90\% confidence interval under a chi-squared estimator
spans approximately 1,200--8,500 hrs — a factor of 7 range. The point estimate
is the best available but should not be used for safety-critical decisions at
this sample size. 30--50 failure events are typically required before MTBF
estimates are used in reliability-centred maintenance (RCM) planning.

$R(100) = 96.5\%$ is valid today under the constant-failure-rate assumption.
As the engine approaches its 25,000-hr life limit, wear-out effects will
increase the hazard rate and this figure will overestimate true reliability.

\section{FMEA Risk Priority Number — Engine}

\begin{align}
  S   &= 9 \quad \text{(engine failure: safety-critical)} \\
  P   &= \lceil (100 - 86) / 100 \times 10 \rceil = \lceil 1.4 \rceil = 2 \\
  D   &= 5 \quad \text{(degradation moderately detectable at current health)} \\[4pt]
  \text{RPN} &= 9 \times 2 \times 5 = \mathbf{90 \text{ (LOW)}}
\end{align}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Engineering judgment:} RPN = 90 correctly classifies this as
LOW priority at the current health score. The limitation is that RPN scores a
snapshot, not a trend. An RPN of 90 with worsening vibration and declining oil
pressure carries higher near-term risk than the same RPN score on a stable
aircraft. I would maintain the LOW classification for formal tracking but add a
non-routine monitoring flag for the correlated vibration + oil pressure pattern.

\section{Landing Gear Fatigue Life}

\[
\text{FAT} = \frac{9{,}712}{50{,}000} \times 100 = \mathbf{19.4\%}
\]

Remaining cycles: $50{,}000 - 9{,}712 = 40{,}288$. At current utilisation
(9,800 cycles over $\approx 8$ years $\approx 1{,}225$ cycles/year), the
structural life limit would not be reached for $\approx 33$ years — well beyond
any realistic service life. The binding life limit for AC-102 is the engine
(RUL = 9,451 hrs), not the airframe structure.

\chapter{Overall Engineering Assessment — AC-102}

The headline metric — Engine Health 86\% — places AC-102 in the LOW risk
category. This classification is defensible, and it would be the correct entry
in a maintenance tracking system.

What the headline does not show is the combination of two parameters, both
healthy in isolation but both trending toward their warning thresholds:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Engine vibration at VSI 2.10 (ISO Zone B, boundary at 2.8) —
        0.70 mm/s margin to the unsatisfactory threshold.
  \item Oil pressure at 51 PSI — only 6 PSI above the warning threshold of
        45 PSI.
\end{itemize}

Together, these are consistent with early bearing wear, a failure mode where
vibration increases and oil pressure decreases simultaneously as the bearing
surface degrades. A weighted-average health score is dragged upward by the
four healthy sensors and can obscure this pattern.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Recommended actions:}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Maintain LOW risk classification for formal scheduling.
  \item Flag for borescope inspection at the next scheduled C-check.
  \item Establish a vibration and oil pressure trend plot; define an
        intervention threshold at VSI 2.5 and oil pressure 48 PSI.
  \item If either parameter crosses its warning threshold before the next
        scheduled inspection, escalate to HIGH and schedule within 50 FH.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Primary life limit:} Engine RUL = 9,451 FH (stress-adjusted).
\\
\textbf{Non-binding:} Landing gear (19.4\% fatigue), electrical, hydraulic —
all nominal.

\chapter{Model Validation and Sanity Checks}

\section{Unit Tests}

The implementation includes 18 automated unit tests covering all calculation
modules (health score, RUL, reliability, risk). These are property-based sanity
checks, not empirical validation against real fleet data:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Scores are bounded to $[0, 100]$.
  \item Reliability $R(t) \in (0, 1]$ for all $t \geq 0$.
  \item $R(t) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$.
  \item Stress factor $SF \geq 1.0$ for all physically-possible sensor readings.
  \item RUL is non-negative (floored at 0).
  \item RPN $\in [1, 1000]$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Dimensional Analysis}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Health scores: dimensionless, $[0, 100]$ — confirmed.
  \item $\lambda$: units of $\text{hr}^{-1}$ — confirmed (MTBF in hours,
        $\lambda = 1/\text{MTBF}$, $\lambda t$ is dimensionless).
  \item RUL: units of flight hours — confirmed ($FH \times SF$ cancels to hours,
        $L_{rated}$ in hours).
  \item VSI: dimensionless ratio — confirmed (both numerator and denominator in mm/s).
\end{itemize}

\section{Limiting Behaviour}

\begin{itemize}
  \item At $FH = 0$: usage score $= 100$, stress factor $= 1.0$, RUL $= L_{rated}$. Correct.
  \item At $FH = L_{rated}$: RUL $= 0$ (floored). Correct.
  \item At nominal sensor values: all scores $= 100$, $SF = 1.0$. Correct.
  \item At critical sensor values: all scores $\approx 40$, $SF > 1.0$. Correct.
  \item At $t = 0$: $R(0) = e^0 = 1.0$. Correct.
\end{itemize}

% ============================================================
\chapter{Limitations}
\label{ch:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{No real sensor data.} All telemetry is procedurally generated
        from seeded pseudo-random functions with banded noise. Model parameters
        are not calibrated to any real aircraft type or failure dataset.

  \item \textbf{Exponential reliability model.} The exponential distribution
        assumes a constant hazard rate. Real aircraft components exhibit
        wear-out behaviour (increasing hazard rate in late life) better
        described by a Weibull distribution with shape parameter $\beta > 1$.
        The exponential model is optimistic for components approaching their
        life limits.

  \item \textbf{Scalar stress factor.} The stress factor is an engineering
        approximation, not a validated degradation model. Production systems
        would use physics-of-failure models (Paris law for fatigue crack
        propagation, Arrhenius for thermal degradation) calibrated against
        material test data and fleet history.

  \item \textbf{MTBF confidence.} With $N < 10$ failure events per aircraft,
        MTBF estimates carry wide confidence intervals. The point estimate is
        used throughout; the uncertainty is not propagated to downstream
        reliability or risk calculations.

  \item \textbf{Static sensor weights.} Subsystem health score weights are
        fixed engineering judgments, not data-derived. In a production PHM
        system, weights would be determined by regression against known failure
        outcomes or by formal importance analysis.

  \item \textbf{No sensor cross-correlation.} Health scores treat each sensor
        independently. Correlated failure signatures (e.g., simultaneous
        vibration increase and oil pressure decrease as discussed for AC-102)
        are not explicitly modelled and can be masked by the weighted average.

  \item \textbf{Regulatory compliance.} This system does not meet the
        requirements for airworthiness-relevant maintenance decision support
        under FAA AC 120-66B, EASA Part-M, or any applicable MRB / MSG-3
        process. It is an educational proof-of-concept only.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Recommendations for Production Implementation}

If this proof-of-concept were to be developed into a production-grade PHM
system, the following engineering steps would be required:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Real data acquisition:} Interface with certified aircraft data
        bus standards (ARINC 429, ARINC 664/AFDX, or direct QAR download).
        Ensure data traceability and timestamp synchronisation.

  \item \textbf{Weibull fitting:} Replace the exponential model with a Weibull
        distribution fitted to fleet failure history. Estimate shape parameter
        $\beta$ and scale parameter $\eta$ using maximum likelihood estimation.
        Use Bayesian updating to incorporate new failures as they occur.

  \item \textbf{Degradation model calibration:} Replace linear interpolation
        with physics-of-failure or data-driven degradation curves (e.g., Paris
        law for fatigue, Arrhenius for temperature-driven failures). Validate
        against known failure events.

  \item \textbf{Prognostic uncertainty quantification:} All RUL estimates
        should carry confidence intervals, not just point estimates. Monte Carlo
        propagation of sensor uncertainty into RUL distributions is the minimum
        acceptable standard.

  \item \textbf{FMEA formalisation:} Replace the simplified RPN model with a
        formally reviewed FMEA / FMECA per ARP 4761. Assign severity, probability,
        and detectability per failure mode, not per subsystem.

  \item \textbf{Regulatory approval:} Submit to applicable regulatory authority
        (FAA DER, EASA DOA) for approval as a health management system under the
        relevant certification basis. Comply with DO-178C (software) and
        DO-254 (hardware) as applicable.

  \item \textbf{Operator integration:} Integrate with airline Maintenance
        Management System (MMS) / MRO system for automatic work order generation,
        parts provisioning, and regulatory compliance tracking.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Conclusions}

This proof-of-concept demonstrates that a coherent, traceable PHM system can
be constructed from fundamental reliability engineering principles without
proprietary tools or fleet data. The explicit calculation chain — from raw
sensor reading through normalised score, weighted composite, stress-adjusted
RUL, exponential reliability, and FMEA RPN — is fully reproducible from the
formulas in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} and verified by the worked example
in Chapter~\ref{ch:worked}.

The engineering value of the system is not its predictive accuracy (which
cannot be validated without real data) but its interpretability. Every
maintenance recommendation produced by the system can be traced back to a
specific sensor reading, a specific formula, and a specific engineering
judgment call. This auditability is a prerequisite for any PHM system
seeking regulatory acceptance.

The worked example for AC-102 illustrates the most important limitation of
composite health scoring: a weighted average can return a reassuring headline
number while masking a correlated degradation pattern that, in a real fleet
context, would warrant closer attention. This is not a flaw in the model —
it is an argument for displaying the individual sensor inputs alongside the
composite score, which the software interface does.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Statement of educational intent:}
This project demonstrates the ability to select appropriate engineering models,
apply them correctly, recognise their assumptions and limitations, and draw
engineering judgments from the results — not just execute calculations. The
gap between this proof-of-concept and a production PHM system is well-defined
and has been explicitly documented above.
